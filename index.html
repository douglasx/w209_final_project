Mass Shootings - An Unbiased View


Modern Mass Shootings are happening every year and seem to be constantly in the news. In today's social climate, it feels like the media is politicizing events and there is a growing distrust of media. This combination has led people to form their own biases against events they see on the news.

Our purpose:

Create an interactive visualization to let you form your own opinions and see unbiased facts/comparisons, though we will offer our insights as to why certain issues are more complicated.


Our Approach:


1. Modify Mass shooting definition

Mass shootings are defined by the FBI as "mass murder" as "four or more murdered during an event with no "cooling-off period" between the murders." However, this encapsulates a lot of other events that are not reported in the media, so weâ€™ve decided to provide visualizations which will allow you to enter your own definition of a mass murder.

2. Remove Mass shooting related biases

By defining your own way of looking at the mass shooting data, we are trying to remove the biases that news media usually have on mass shooting. These biases can range anywhere from demographic, weapon types, and location of events.

Data

  • Stanford MSA Database: the database contains all the reported mass shooting events between 1960 and 2016. The database contains detailed description of the different aspects of the mass shooting events including: number of victims, shooter's description, weapon descriptions, etc.
  • US demographic data: The data source features ethnicity distribution in the US from 1990 to 2016.
  • US firearms data: The firearms manufactured, imported and exported in the US from 1990 to 2016.

Users

  • Gun control policy makers: The tool can be used by US gun control policy makers to look at the problem from multiple angles.
  • Gun control promoters and activists: Promoters and activists, including NGOs, can direct their focus on specific aspects of gun violence, and potentially offer viable solutions based on the insights from this tool.
  • Curious Individuals: Anyone looking to inform themselves further about the issue can use this tool!

Tasks

  1. Present the overview of mass shooting violence in the US, allowing for custom definition of mass shootings based on victims.
  2. Focus on the ethnicity distribution in the US population versus the overall race of the shooter to show there is no racial bias towards mass shootings.
  3. Focus on the firearms and weapon types that are the most deadly.

Between 1990 and 2016, the breakdown of the shooter's race is similar to the demographic distribution across the US.






Insights:


Insight 1

From the statistics between 1990 and 2016, no ethnic group seems to be particularly more prone to cause mass shooting.

Insight 2

Mass shootings where the shooter is African American are scattered more towards the East and South of the country.

Insight 3

There have been some US States where no mass shoooting was reported between 1990-2016.

Insight 4

Wednesday is the day with the highest number of reported mass shootings, while Saturday with the least.


Insight 5

A bar chart showing the frequency of events during each day of a week.

Insight 6

The age groups 33-42 and 21-32 are the two dominant age groups of the shooter. However, additional data on the US age distribution is needed before concluding whether people of the age group have higher risk of causing mass shooting.
Moreover, the age group with the highest number of reported mass shootings are slightly different across races.


Potential Action For Gun Control

The higher mass shooting numbers among the age groups 33-42 and 21-32 could be reflecting the difference in the level of economic burden and responsibility that different cultures impose to people of different age groups. A correlation between the culture and likelihood of mass shooting is proposed, and potential social intervention can be applied to reduce the likelihood of mass shooting.













Distributions and Firearms

Below you will see a visualization that shows you a map of the US with a darker saturation showing how deadly the shootings were by state. You can modify the definition of a mass shooting by entering it in the box below Total Number of Victims. The minimum starting number is 3, but you can go all the way up to 102. You can see which years were the most deadly and filter to see exclusively for that year of data.


Insights:


Insight 1

The number of reported mass shooting events did not show a yearly growth trend until after 2011, and the year 2015 and 2016 are particular dramatic.

Insight 2

Our shooting fatality heat map correlates with population density but does not reflect the severity of these shootings.

Insight 3

The US firearm manufacture and import had a peak in 1993, but then went down until another hike starting from 2006. The total number of firearms manufactured and imported in 2016 triples the number of 1990. There are certain correlation between the hike in mass shooting and the increase in firearms.


Potential Action For Gun Control

The correlation between the firearms growth and the hike in mass shooting events since 2006 is strong. However, the mass shooting data alone will not be enough to make a conclusion for causality. The details into the firearms, including the types of firearms ownership is needed for indepth analysis.
